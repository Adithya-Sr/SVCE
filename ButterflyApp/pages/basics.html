Life Cycle Of Butterfly

A butterfly’s life begins with the egg stage which is laid on the stems and leaves of plants followed by the active feeding stage known as the caterpillar (larva) where it keeps shedding its skin as it grows and emerges several thousand times bigger before it goes into its resting stage called the pupa (chrysalis).

Here, it spins an enclosure around itself and sleeps until it develops the adult features and transforms itself into a big, beautiful, flying imago (adult).

Butterflies and Moths

The difference between a butterfly and a moth lies in the antennae, colour, resting stance, period of activity and pupa. While butterflies have club-shaped antennae, appear brighter in colour, rest with their wings held together, active during the day with the pupa being a chrysalis which clings to a branch for support; moths have feathery antennae, usually dull in appearance, rest with their wings open, active during the night with the pupa being a cocoon on the ground. Parts of a Butterfly

  • 1:Fore wing

    Present on the anterior region, roughly triangular in shape

  • 2:Antennae

    Wire-like structures present on the top of the head forming the sensory appendage, segmented with club shaped ends; used for smell, balance and orientation

  • 3:Head

    Consists of a pair of compound eyes, a proboscis and a pair of antennae

  • 4:Proboscis

    The slender, flexible, tube-like mouthpart (two tubes joined together) that is kept coiled when not in use. Whenever a butterfly finds its food, it extends its proboscis to suck the food

  • 5:Legs

    Three pairs of legs each consisting of the femur, the tibia and the tarsus with claws in the ends are used for walking, climbing, tasting and holding onto things

  • 6:Abdomen

    Hind part of the body holding most of the vital organs for the butterfly’s survival - the heart, the reproductive organs, the digestive tract and the spiracles (breathing pores)

  • 7:Hind wing

    Present on the posterior region, fan shaped or shell shaped

  • 8:Eyespots

    Eye like spots present on the wing. (Tornal spot)

  • 9:Tail

    Slender projection of the hind wing

  • 10:Costa:

    Leading edge of a wing.

  • 11:Apex

    Fore wing tip

  • 12:Underside

    The bottom view or the ventral side of a butterfly

  • 13:Upperside

    The top view or the dorsal side of a butterfly

    • Diversity

    India serves as a host to 1800 species of butterflies which are classified as follows:

    Named after their way of flight, the ‘Skippers’ or Hesperiidae are a family which have certain characteristics resembling moths. Darts, Awls, and Swifts are categorised under this family.

    The second-largest family is Lycaenidae or the ‘Blues and coppers’ or ‘Gossamer-winged butterflies’ has species like Grass Jewel, Gram Blue and Sunbeam

    The ‘Brush-footed butterflies or four-footed butterflies’ or Nymphalidae is the largest family. They have forelegs which are never fully developed or functional as in the other families. The popular species include Tigers, Crows and Bushbrowns

    The ‘Whites and yellows’ or Pieridae is a large family consisting of butterflies that have white, yellow, orange or sulphur colouration. Emigrants, Pioneers and Grass Yellows fall under this family

    The Papilionidae or ‘Swallowtail’ family are the largest (in size) and of attractive colours such as the Limes, the Mormons and the Roses. Most of them have tailed hind wings and hence the name

    Behaviour

    Although a majority of butterflies visit flowers for their nectar to obtain sugars for energy, there is a minority of butterflies which feed on rotting fruits, pollen, dead and decaying matter, dung, tree sap and other organic material. The mud-puddling behaviour can be seen in species like the Common Emigrant, Common Lime and Common Crow where the butterflies of the same or different species feed gregariously on wet soil, to obtain the minerals, salt and nutrients from the soil vital for reproduction

    Some other species orient themselves together for basking while some are choosy in their perches and use the same perch for basking. Many species exhibit polymorphisms such as sexual dimorphism, mimicry (defense mechanism) and seasonal polyphenism (dry season and wet season forms). Some are capable of making clicking sounds and have a good vision and hearing ability

    Defense Mechanisms

    Butterflies are very fragile, almost defenseless and vulnerable creatures. They fall prey to spiders, insects, frogs, reptiles, birds and small mammals They have developed various strategies to protect themselves from these predators. Some caterpillars eat plants that are poisonous to their predators but can sequester the toxins themselves since they have better digestive mechanisms (automimicry)

    The colours of the caterpillar and the adult butterflies also play a major role in protection by camouflaging and displaying bright and warning colours (aposematism). Many caterpillars blend with the colours of their host plants while others look like bird droppings (example: Common Mormon). The hairy structures on the larval body also protect them. The adults also have colours that blend with their environment as in the case of Common Evening Brown and Common Bushbrown

    Habitat

    The habitat where a butterfly lives and breeds depends upon various factors such as:

    • The species of the plant
    • Nativity of the plant species
    • The species of the butterfly
    • Availability of ample sunlight and moisture
    • Time of the day or year
    • The location of a suitable habitat
    • The presence of both their larval and nectar host plants to thrive upon
